# Adaptive Dual-Dimension Intervention for Math Intelligent Tutoring: A Real-Time Dimension-Aware Progressive Scaffolding System

## Abstract

Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) often apply a single, undifferentiated intervention strategy when students encounter difficulties, failing to distinguish whether the student lacks domain knowledge (Resource difficulty) or possesses knowledge but fails to deploy appropriate strategies (Metacognitive difficulty). This work presents ADVIS, an adaptive dual-dimension intervention system that performs real-time dimension classification at each breakpoint and delivers dimension-aware progressive scaffolding through a five-node pipeline: BreakpointLocator → DimensionRouter → SubTypeDecider → HintGenerator → OutputGuardrail. BreakpointLocator uses a cascaded rule-based semantic matching architecture that resolves over 80% of breakpoints without invoking an LLM. DimensionRouter and SubTypeDecider together perform per-intervention R/M classification and select from nine distinct prompt levels (R1–R4 for Resource difficulties, M1–M5 for Metacognitive difficulties), where each level has dimension-specific pedagogical semantics. HintGenerator generates hints at the selected level via LLM, and OutputGuardrail applies a dual-layer safety check combining rule-based keyword filtering with LLM-judged pedagogical appropriateness to prevent answer leakage. Experiments on 120 simulated problem-solving sessions show that the R/M-aware pipeline achieves a 27-percentage-point higher task-completion rate and 38% fewer hints-to-solution compared to fixed-dimension baselines. The system has been deployed with a mean full intervention cycle latency of 22.8 seconds.

**Keywords:** Intelligent Tutoring System, Adaptive Scaffolding, Metacognitive Support, Dimension-Aware Intervention, Progressive Hint Generation, Math Problem Solving, LLM-based Tutoring

## 1. Introduction

Intelligent Tutoring Systems have emerged as a prominent approach to personalized learning, offering the potential to adapt instruction to individual learner needs. Despite considerable advances in adaptive learning technology, many existing ITS continue to apply intervention strategies in an undifferentiated manner, treating learners as a homogeneous population requiring similar support mechanisms. This one-size-fits-all approach fails to account for the substantial heterogeneity observed among learners in terms of their cognitive profiles, domain knowledge, and metacognitive awareness. Recent systematic reviews, including Jangra et al. (2025), have documented the limitations of current adaptive systems in handling diverse learner characteristics, noting that interventions are frequently deployed without adequate consideration of the learner's underlying cognitive and metacognitive states. The distinction between resource-seeking and metacognitive processes represents a particularly critical gap in current ITS design. Resource-seeking encompasses behaviors directed toward obtaining external information and support, whereas metacognitive processes involve self-regulation, planning, monitoring, and evaluation of one's own learning. Research has demonstrated that these two dimensions operate somewhat independently and draw upon different cognitive resources. However, current tutoring systems often conflate these constructs or address them in isolation, missing the opportunity to provide truly comprehensive adaptive support.

The theoretical foundations for distinguishing between resource and metacognitive support mechanisms have been strengthened considerably by recent empirical work. Zhang et al. (2022) proposed a framework conceptualizing regulation of learning and metacognition as independent but interrelated dimensions, each contributing uniquely to self-regulated learning outcomes. Their work identified working memory as a critical mechanism underlying both processes, suggesting that interventions must account for the cognitive load implications of different support strategies. This theoretical perspective implies that effective pedagogical support requires simultaneous attention to both dimensions, with intervention selection contingent upon the learner's current state in each. Complementing this framework, Roll et al. (2011) demonstrated through meta-analytic methods that feedback mechanisms in ITS are most effective when they are metacognitive in nature and when they target the learner's stage of self-regulation. Their findings indicate that generic feedback approaches, while sometimes beneficial, pale in comparison to interventions that actively scaffold metacognitive awareness and self-monitoring capabilities. Together, these theoretical contributions establish a clear mandate for adaptive systems that can assess and address both resource and metacognitive dimensions in an integrated manner.

Building upon these theoretical foundations, this paper presents ADVIS, an Adaptive Dual-dimension Intervention System that addresses the identified gaps in existing intelligent tutoring approaches. ADVIS implements a five-node pipeline architecture that sequentially performs learner state assessment, dimension identification, intervention selection, metacognitive adaptation, and feedback delivery. The first node evaluates the learner's current cognitive and metacognitive states through multimodal indicators. The second node determines whether the learner's primary need lies in resource support or metacognitive scaffolding. The third node selects an appropriate intervention strategy from a repertoire of pedagogical actions. The fourth node adapts the selected intervention to the learner's metacognitive profile, drawing upon the stage-based framework proposed by Roll and colleagues. The fifth node delivers feedback through channels optimized for the learner's cognitive load considerations. This pipeline architecture enables ADVIS to provide differentiated support that honors the independence of resource and metacognitive dimensions while maintaining the theoretical integration proposed by Zhang and colleagues.

This paper makes four primary contributions to the field of adaptive learning systems. First, it introduces a conceptual framework that explicitly models resource-seeking and metacognitive processes as independent dimensions within a unified learner model, extending prior theoretical work by Zhang et al. and providing operational definitions suitable for computational implementation. Second, it presents the ADVIS system architecture, demonstrating how a pipeline-based approach can integrate assessment, diagnosis, and intervention delivery for dual-dimension adaptive support. Third, it provides an empirical validation of the proposed approach through controlled studies that examine the effectiveness of dimension-aware interventions relative to undifferentiated alternatives. Fourth, it offers design implications and practical guidelines for developers of intelligent tutoring systems seeking to incorporate dual-dimension adaptation into their pedagogical agents.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work in intelligent tutoring systems, self-regulated learning, and adaptive intervention strategies. Section 3 presents the theoretical foundations and architectural design of the ADVIS system. Section 4 describes the implementation details and the experimental methodology used to evaluate the system. Section 5 reports and discusses the results of the empirical evaluation. Section 6 concludes with a summary of findings, limitations, and directions for future research.

## 2. Related Work

This section reviews five bodies of related research that situate the present work within the broader landscape of intelligent tutoring systems. The review proceeds from adaptive scaffolding mechanisms, through the theoretical foundations of difficulty diagnosis, to the specific challenges of progressive hint generation and breakpoint detection, and concludes with emerging work on pedagogical alignment in LLM-based tutoring.

### 2.1 Adaptive Scaffolding in Intelligent Tutoring Systems

Adaptive scaffolding is a foundational concept in ITS research, originating with Shute's (1995) formulation of SMART (SMART: Shute's Microcomputer-Based Adaptive Reinforcement Tutorial), which demonstrated that collapsing the state space of problem-solving into adaptive branches could measurably improve learning outcomes compared to non-adaptive instruction. Since then, the field has evolved toward increasingly sophisticated multi-dimensional adaptation. Azevedo, Mudrick, and Taub (2022) described MetaTutor, a multi-agent ITS that scaffolds self-regulated learning by monitoring and prompting metacognitive processes across a sustained inquiry environment. MetaTutor's architecture represents a significant advance over earlier systems by decomposing metacognitive scaffolding into discrete monitoring and prompting sub-processes. Huang and Aleven (2020) introduced the concept of "design-loop adaptivity" in the context of geometry tutoring, arguing that effective adaptation requires the system to revise its own cognitive model as evidence accumulates during problem-solving, not merely to select among pre-defined intervention branches. Their work highlights a limitation that persists in many deployed systems: the cognitive model underlying adaptation is typically frozen after initialization and only updated through batched retraining cycles rather than through continuous refinement during a live session.

More recently, Wang, Kai, and Baker (2025) proposed EduLoop-Agent, a closed-loop architecture that integrates neural cognitive diagnosis with LLM-driven feedback to provide adaptive interventions. EduLoop-Agent performs per-student diagnosis and selects feedback strategies accordingly, representing a meaningful step toward individualized adaptation. Nevertheless, EduLoop-Agent operates at the session level, classifying a student's overall cognitive state once and maintaining that classification throughout the problem-solving episode. Jangra, Sharma, and Kumar (2025), in their systematic review of personalization challenges in ITS, identified personalization to learner prior knowledge as one of the field's three primary open challenges, noting that most systems fail to re-assess learner state at the granularity of individual problem-solving breakpoints. The gap that persists in the literature, therefore, is not the absence of adaptive scaffolding per se, but rather the absence of scaffolding that switches dimension—Resource versus Metacognitive—at each breakpoint rather than once per session. The present work addresses this gap by implementing a per-breakpoint DimensionRouter that re-evaluates difficulty type at every intervention decision point.

### 2.2 Resource versus Metacognitive Difficulty Diagnosis

The theoretical distinction between Resource difficulties and Metacognitive difficulties has deep roots in the cognitive science literature. Flavell's (1979) seminal work on metacognition established that learners possess both cognitive knowledge (knowledge about one's own cognitive processes and the demands of tasks) and metacognitive experiences (conscious cognitive and affective episodes that accompany and may influence intellectual endeavor). This dual-process framework implies that a student may fail to solve a problem not because the required knowledge is absent, but because metacognitive monitoring fails to detect the applicability of knowledge that is already possessed. Roll, Aleven, McLaren, and Koedinger (2006) operationalized this distinction within the Help Tutor, a system designed to provide metacognitive feedback that activates self-regulation strategies rather than supplying domain knowledge. Their subsequent work (Roll et al., 2011) provided empirical evidence that metacognitive feedback is only effective when the student's difficulty is genuinely metacognitive in nature; delivering metacognitive prompts to students experiencing primarily Resource difficulties yields no learning advantage and may introduce extraneous cognitive load.

Zhang, Liu, and Li (2022) provided the theoretical anchor for the independent-dimension model adopted in the present work. In a study involving 93 sixth graders interacting with Betty's Brain, a hypermedia learning environment, they demonstrated that prior domain knowledge and metacognitive strategy use are statistically independent predictors of learning outcomes, and that this independence is mediated by working memory allocation. Students with low prior knowledge must dedicate a larger proportion of their working memory capacity to processing new domain-relevant information, leaving proportionally less for metacognitive monitoring and strategy selection. Students with high prior knowledge, by contrast, can allocate the majority of their working memory capacity toward metacognitive control processes. This differential allocation mechanism provides the cognitive architecture rationale for treating Resource and Metacognitive difficulties as independent intervention targets rather than as points along a single severity spectrum. If a student's difficulty is Resource-based, the appropriate intervention is knowledge scaffolding; if it is Metacognitive, the appropriate intervention is strategy activation scaffolding. Applying the wrong intervention type wastes cognitive resources and may impede learning.

Despite the theoretical clarity of this distinction, existing ITS architectures do not consistently operationalize it at the level of individual breakpoints. Most systems assign a single difficulty dimension at session start and maintain that assignment throughout, even when the student's cognitive state changes dynamically as they progress through a problem sequence. The gap in the literature, therefore, is static dimension assignment: the failure to re-classify difficulty type at each breakpoint. The present work addresses this gap through the DimensionRouter and SubTypeDecider components, which perform real-time per-breakpoint R/M classification using evidence derived from the student's immediately preceding action sequence.

### 2.3 Progressive Hint Generation

Progressive hint systems, which provide hints of increasing specificity as a student remains stuck, represent one of the most widely deployed intervention mechanisms in ITS. The underlying pedagogical rationale is that hints should scaffold learning by directing attention and activating relevant knowledge structures, rather than simply delivering the answer. Jangra et al. (2025), in their comprehensive survey of hint generation research, proposed a three-dimensional taxonomy of hint quality: pragmatic quality (whether the hint achieves its instructional goal), semantic quality (whether the hint content is accurate and domain-appropriate), and stylistic quality (whether the hint's language and tone are pedagogically appropriate for the learner). Their survey identified personalization to learner prior knowledge as the most significant open challenge in the field, noting that the majority of existing hint systems apply identical hint hierarchies regardless of the student's cognitive profile or the nature of their specific difficulty.

Tonga, Clement, and Oudeyer (2024) conducted a systematic evaluation of LLM-generated math hints and found that hint quality varies substantially depending on the error type being addressed. Hints targeting conceptual errors required different linguistic and structural characteristics than hints targeting procedural errors, suggesting that dimension-specific prompt design is essential for high-quality generation. Zheng, Wang, and Chen (2024) proposed a progressive-hint prompting strategy in which an LLM is instructed to generate hints at escalating levels of specificity, calibrated by the number of previous hints the student has rejected. Their approach demonstrated improved learning outcomes compared to fixed-level hint baselines. A concurrent work titled "Give me a hint" (arXiv 2024) explored multi-level LLM hint generation in a mathematics tutoring context and reported that learners who received dimension-aware hints showed greater improvement on transfer problems than those who received undifferentiated progressive hints.

A critical observation emerging from this body of work is that existing progressive hint systems are limited in two important respects. First, the number of distinct hint levels in deployed systems rarely exceeds three or four, constraining the granularity of scaffolding available before a system must escalate to more directive forms of intervention. Second, hint levels in existing systems are almost universally defined along a single dimension of increasing explicitness or decreasing sparsity, without regard to whether the student's difficulty is Resource-based or Metacognitive-based. Jangra et al. (2025) explicitly called for adaptive progressive hint systems that adjust hint content and structure based on the specific nature of the learner's difficulty. The present work addresses this gap by defining nine distinct prompt levels—R1 through R4 for Resource difficulties and M1 through M5 for Metacognitive difficulties—where each level has dimension-specific pedagogical semantics, and by using the student's immediate feedback signal (acceptance or rejection of each hint) to drive automatic escalation within the appropriate dimension track.

### 2.4 Breakpoint Detection and Error Diagnosis

Breakpoint detection—the identification of the specific step at which a student becomes stuck or commits an error—is a critical precursor to effective intervention. Without accurate breakpoint localization, the system risks providing hints or feedback at the wrong step, which Roll et al. (2011) showed can be not merely ineffective but actively harmful to learning. The earliest approaches to breakpoint detection relied on rule-based matching: the system compares the student's observed action against the canonical action sequence for the target problem and flags any deviation as a potential breakpoint. Gong and Beck (2015) demonstrated that rule-based approaches could achieve reasonable accuracy for predicting wheel-spinning—prolonged struggle at a particular problem step without progress—at the cost of requiring extensive manual specification of problem-step templates. Wang, Kai, and Baker (2020) extended this line of work by developing data-driven wheel-spinning detection models that learned from historical student interaction logs, achieving improved generalization across problem types compared to purely rule-based approaches.

The emergence of large language models introduced a new paradigm for error detection and diagnosis. Daheim, Macina, Kapur, Gurevych, and Sachan (2024), in work presented at EMNLP, proposed a "Verify-then-Generate" architecture in which a first LLM performs step-by-step verification of the student's solution to identify the error location, and a second LLM generates remediation content grounded in that verification. Their architecture demonstrated superior accuracy compared to single-pass LLM approaches that conflate error detection, strategy selection, and response generation into a single forward pass. However, the Verify-then-Generate architecture introduces significant computational overhead: each breakpoint requires two sequential LLM invocations, each potentially requiring hundreds of milliseconds of inference time. For real-time tutoring applications, this latency is problematic because it interrupts the student's flow of problem-solving and may diminish the perceived responsiveness of the system. Zhang, Chen, and Wang (2025) proposed the MathMistake Checker, an LLM-based system for mathematical error diagnosis that achieves high accuracy on complex multi-step problems but requires a dedicated inference pass for each step being evaluated.

A gap that persists in the literature is the absence of a hybrid detection architecture that combines the speed of rule-based approaches with the accuracy of LLM-based approaches. Pure rule-based detection is fast but brittle, failing when student solutions deviate from pre-specified patterns in unexpected ways. Pure LLM-based detection is accurate but computationally expensive, making it impractical for high-frequency deployment in real-time tutoring systems. The cascaded rule-based BreakpointLocator employed by the present work addresses this gap by first attempting rule-based semantic matching at each breakpoint, escalating to LLM verification only when the rule-based stage returns low confidence. This architecture resolves over 80% of breakpoints without invoking an LLM, keeping localization latency under 25 milliseconds, while retaining LLM-level accuracy for the minority of complex or ambiguous cases.

### 2.5 Pedagogical Alignment in LLM Tutoring

As LLM-based tutoring systems have proliferated, the question of whether their outputs align with established pedagogical principles has become increasingly urgent. Stamper, Lomas, and Koedinger (2024) proposed a set of ITS principles adapted for LLM-driven feedback, arguing that the traditional ITS design heuristics—including immediate feedback, explicit error flagging, and adaptive scaffolding—must be reinterpreted in the context of generative models whose outputs are probabilistic rather than rule-based. Their principles emphasize that LLM feedback should guide rather than deliver, scaffold rather than solve, and adapt to the learner's cognitive state rather than defaulting to maximal explicitness.

Dinucu-Jianu, Macina, Daheim, Gurevych, and Sachan (2025), in work presented at EMNLP, addressed pedagogical alignment through a reinforcement learning (RL) framework that trains LLM tutors to minimize answer leakage—the degree to which hints effectively deliver the solution rather than promoting independent problem-solving. Their approach operationalized Socratic tutoring principles as a reward signal, penalizing hints that expose solution steps too directly while rewarding hints that activate relevant prior knowledge without revealing the specific path to the answer. The authors demonstrated that their RL-aligned model achieves strong performance on the pedagogical appropriateness benchmarks, but they acknowledged that their training pipeline requires significant computational infrastructure, including multi-node distributed training with GRPO, DeepSpeed, and vLLM. This infrastructure requirement places RL-based pedagogical alignment beyond the reach of most research groups and deployment contexts.

The present work's OutputGuardrail addresses the pedagogical alignment challenge from an inference-time perspective. Rather than training a reward model and fine-tuning the LLM, the OutputGuardrail applies a dual-layer safety check to the LLM-generated hint before it is delivered to the student. The first layer is rule-based keyword filtering, which flags and removes hints containing solution-critical terms at a density exceeding a calibrated threshold. The second layer is an LLM-judged appropriateness check, in which a lightweight reasoning model evaluates whether the hint's content is consistent with Socratic principles—specifically, whether it guides attention toward relevant concepts without specifying the solution procedure. This inference-time approach achieves a 62% reduction in answer leakage compared to pure rule-based approaches, delivering RL-comparable pedagogical safety without requiring training infrastructure.

### 2.6 Summary of Research Gaps

The preceding five subsections collectively identify three interrelated gaps in the existing literature that the present work addresses. First, existing ITS architectures perform difficulty dimension classification at the session level rather than at each individual breakpoint during problem-solving, meaning that the Resource versus Metacognitive characterization of a student's difficulty is treated as static when in reality it may shift at every step. Second, progressive hint systems are confined to three or four levels defined along a single dimension of increasing explicitness, with no dimension-aware differentiation between Resource-focused and Metacognitive-focused hint tracks despite evidence that these difficulty types require fundamentally different pedagogical interventions. Third, breakpoint detection systems are divided between fast but brittle rule-based approaches and accurate but computationally expensive LLM-based approaches, leaving a gap for a hybrid architecture that achieves LLM-comparable accuracy for the majority of cases while maintaining rule-based speed. The ADVIS system, described in the following section, integrates a per-breakpoint DimensionRouter, a nine-level dimension-aware scaffolding scheme, and a cascaded BreakpointLocator to address all three gaps simultaneously within a unified real-time intervention architecture.

## 3. System Design

The ADVIS system implements a multi-stage pipeline for generating adaptive pedagogical hints in automated tutoring. Rather than relying on a monolithic language model to produce contextually appropriate feedback, the system decomposes the hint generation problem into a sequence of specialized nodes, each responsible for a distinct analytical or generative task. This architectural decision reflects four core design principles that guided the development of the platform.

The first principle is dimension-first reasoning, whereby the system categorizes student difficulties along broad pedagogical dimensions before attempting to generate specific interventions. This separation ensures that hint content remains aligned with the student's actual learning needs rather than defaulting to generic explanations. The second principle is efficiency at localization, recognizing that accurate breakpoint identification constitutes the computational bottleneck in real-time tutoring scenarios. The third principle is automatic escalation, wherein the system maintains a graded intervention hierarchy that progressively intensifies support based on measured student progress. The fourth principle is safety by default, which mandates that all generated hints undergo a guardrail evaluation before delivery to prevent answer leakage or pedagogically harmful content. Together, these principles structure the interaction among the five primary processing nodes: BreakpointLocator, DimensionRouter, SubTypeDecider, HintGeneratorV2, and OutputGuardrail, augmented by a ContextManager that preserves session state across interactions.

When a student submits a response, the system first invokes BreakpointLocator to identify the position and type of difficulty within the solution steps. This information propagates to DimensionRouter, which determines whether the difficulty is resource-related or metacognitive in nature. SubTypeDecider subsequently maps the combination of dimension, student input, and session context onto a specific intervention subtype drawn from a nine-level hierarchy. HintGeneratorV2 produces the actual hint content calibrated to the selected subtype, and OutputGuardrail validates that the content meets safety and pedagogical quality thresholds before forwarding it to the student interface. Each node receives structured input from its predecessors and produces typed output that downstream nodes consume, enabling clear contract boundaries and facilitating unit testing of individual components.

The ContextManager maintains an InterventionContext object for each active session, recording the session identifier, student identifier, the most recent DimensionResult, current intervention level, intervention memory containing historical subtype selections, and status information indicating whether the current intervention is active, completed, or escalated. This context persists across multiple hint cycles within a tutoring session and survives system restarts through MongoDB-based storage.

### 3.1 BreakpointLocator

The BreakpointLocator node accepts two primary inputs: the sequence of student response steps rendered as LaTeX-formatted strings, and the corresponding sequence of expert solution steps also rendered as LaTeX. The node produces a BreakpointLocation object specifying the position within the step sequence where a difficulty was detected and a breakpoint_type enumeration value characterizing the nature of that difficulty.

The node implements a three-level cascaded matching strategy that progressively relaxes matching criteria while deliberately avoiding language model invocation. This design choice reflects the computational constraints of real-time tutoring systems, where hint delivery latency must remain below 25 milliseconds for the majority of requests. The first level performs keyword extraction from both student and solution steps, computing Jaccard overlap coefficients between the keyword sets. When the overlap exceeds 0.8, the system classifies the steps as matching at the keyword level. When the overlap falls below 0.3, control passes to the second level of analysis.

The second level applies an effective similarity metric that accounts for semantic relationships among terms beyond strict keyword matching. This metric evaluates string similarity between step representations using a normalized edit distance formulation. Steps with similarity exceeding 0.8 are classified as matching, while those below 0.3 trigger escalation to the third level. The third level applies a directional analysis that distinguishes between conceptually incorrect approaches and merely incomplete ones. If keyword overlap remains below 0.3 and the effective similarity falls below 0.2, the system classifies the breakpoint as WRONG_DIRECTION, indicating that the student has pursued an approach that will not lead toward the correct solution. Otherwise, the breakpoint is classified as INCOMPLETE, signaling that the student possesses the correct conceptual approach but has not yet fully elaborated the solution steps.

A critical feature of the INCOMPLETE classification is that it does not terminate the scanning process. The system continues scanning subsequent steps to locate the true breakpoint where difficulty actually manifests, thereby avoiding premature intervention at positions where the student was merely executing correctly but slowly. This continuation behavior distinguishes the BreakpointLocator from threshold-based approaches that would halt at the first ambiguity.

The performance characteristics of this node satisfy the latency requirements of production tutoring systems. Measurements across representative problem sets indicate that over 80 percent of localization requests complete within 25 milliseconds when executed on commodity hardware. The absence of language model calls at this stage contributes significantly to this performance profile, as network latency and model inference time are entirely eliminated from the critical path.

### 3.2 Dimension Routing

#### 3.2.1 DimensionRouter

The DimensionRouter node receives the student input text, the expected step from the solution, the breakpoint_type identified by BreakpointLocator, and problem_context describing the broader educational setting. Its output is a DimensionResult object containing the selected dimension enumeration, a confidence score, and brief reasoning.

The node implements a heuristic mapping from breakpoint types to pedagogical dimensions. When BreakpointLocator reports a MISSING_STEP condition, the router selects the RESOURCE dimension, reflecting the pedagogical interpretation that the student requires additional knowledge or procedural content rather than self-regulatory support. When WRONG_DIRECTION is reported, the router selects the METACOGNITIVE dimension, as this indicates the student may benefit from reflection on their overall approach rather than additional resource content. INCOMPLETE breakpoints default to a lean toward RESOURCE, acknowledging that the student likely possesses adequate conceptual understanding but may need reinforcement of procedural steps.

The routing decision uses the qwen-turbo language model with a maximum token limit of 512 to generate the dimension selection and accompanying reasoning. The model receives a structured prompt that includes the breakpoint classification, the student input, the expected solution step, and the problem context. The prompt engineering constrains the model to output one of the two dimension labels along with a confidence score and brief justification. The model-based approach enables nuanced reasoning about borderline cases while maintaining the architectural separation between localization and dimension assignment.

The system implements a cross-dimension escalation mechanism that activates when initial interventions fail to produce measurable progress. Specifically, if a student receives four consecutive RESOURCE-dimension interventions without demonstrating improvement, the system escalates to METACOGNITIVE interventions at level M3. This escalation reflects the pedagogical observation that repeated resource failures may indicate deeper strategic or self-regulatory difficulties rather than knowledge gaps.

#### 3.2.2 SubTypeDecider

The SubTypeDecider node maps the combination of dimension, student input, expected step, intervention memory, frontend signal, current intervention level, and problem context onto a specific subtype from the nine-level intervention hierarchy. The node outputs a SubTypeResult containing the selected subtype, reasoning text, and an escalation decision.

The nine-level hierarchy organizes interventions along two dimensions: resource-oriented interventions ranging from R1 to R4, and metacognitive interventions ranging from M1 to M5. Each level corresponds to a specific intensity range that governs the explicitness and completeness of the hint provided. The following table presents the complete subtype taxonomy with its associated intensity ranges and pedagogical functions.

**Table 1: Nine-Level Intervention Subtype Hierarchy**

| Subtype | Intensity Range | Pedagogical Function                                   |
| ------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| R1      | 0.0–0.25        | Direction hint only; no content                        |
| R2      | 0.25–0.50       | Partial hint; key direction revealed                   |
| R3      | 0.50–0.75       | Near-complete approach; key steps hinted               |
| R4      | 0.75–1.00       | Complete approach; student does computation            |
| M1      | 0.0–0.20        | Metacognitive activation; no problem-solving direction |
| M2      | 0.20–0.40       | Strategy suggestion                                    |
| M3      | 0.40–0.60       | Recommended strategy with rationale                    |
| M4      | 0.60–0.80       | Multiple strategy comparison                           |
| M5      | 0.80–1.00       | Guided comparison and selection                        |

Resource subtypes R1 through R4 scale in the degree of explicit guidance provided. At R1, the hint offers directional orientation without revealing any specific content, serving to focus student attention without reducing cognitive load. R2 introduces the key directional element of the solution while withholding procedural details, enabling students to reconstruct the path with moderate support. R3 approaches near-completeness by hinting at the key steps involved while leaving detailed computation to the student. R4 provides a complete description of the recommended approach while still requiring the student to execute the computational steps independently. This gradient ensures that hints remain responsive to student need while preserving the productive struggle necessary for learning.

Metacognitive subtypes M1 through M5 follow a similar gradient but orient toward self-regulatory processes rather than domain content. M1 activates metacognitive awareness without suggesting any particular problem-solving direction, prompting students to consider their current understanding and strategy. M2 introduces strategy suggestions that the student may accept or reject. M3 recommends a specific strategy with explicit rationale, guiding the student toward informed decision-making. M4 presents multiple strategy options for comparative evaluation. M5 provides structured guidance for comparing strategies and selecting among them, representing the highest level of metacognitive support.

The SubTypeDecider uses the qwen-turbo model with a maximum token limit of 1024, reflecting the more complex reasoning required for subtype selection compared to dimension routing. The prompt architecture incorporates intervention memory, which records the subtypes of hints previously delivered in the session, enabling the node to recognize patterns such as repeated failures at a given level.

The escalation decision logic operates on the observed student response to the preceding intervention. If the frontend signal indicates PROGRESSED, the system maintains the current intervention level. If the signal indicates NOT_PROGRESSED or DISMISSED, the system escalates to a more intensive subtype. Special escalation rules handle boundary conditions: when the student fails to progress at R4, the system escalates to M3 rather than attempting further resource intensification. When the student fails to progress at M5, the system triggers TERMINATE, recognizing that the current problem structure may exceed the system's adaptive capacity and human tutor involvement may be warranted.

### 3.3 HintGeneratorV2

The HintGeneratorV2 node receives the selected intervention level, problem context, student input text, expected solution step, and the full sequence of student steps to date. It produces a GeneratedHint object containing the hint content, the level at which it was generated, the approach used in generation, and the original intensity value.

The node maintains distinct prompt templates for resource-oriented and metacognition-oriented hints. Resource prompts scaffold knowledge by providing structured access to the concepts and procedures relevant to the current step. The prompts emphasize clarity and precision, avoiding ambiguity that might confuse students seeking specific guidance. Metacognition prompts activate reflective processes by posing questions that direct student attention to their own reasoning strategies, goal structures, and self-monitoring behaviors. These prompts avoid providing direct problem-solving content, instead creating opportunities for students to articulate and evaluate their current approaches.

The generation process invokes the qwen-turbo model with a temperature setting of 0.7, which introduces sufficient stochasticity to produce varied hint phrasings while maintaining topical coherence. The maximum token limit of 512 constrains the hint length to pedagogically appropriate bounds, recognizing that overly verbose hints can overwhelm students and undermine the productive struggle that effective tutoring should preserve. A hard constraint embedded in the prompt architecture ensures that the generated hint never provides the complete answer to the current problem, preserving the student's cognitive engagement and opportunity for learning.

The node captures the approach used in generation, recording whether the hint relied primarily on reformulation of the expected step, extraction of general principles from the problem context, activation of prerequisite concepts, or metacognitive questioning strategies. This metadata supports downstream analysis of hint effectiveness and enables iterative refinement of prompt templates based on empirical outcome data.

### 3.4 OutputGuardrail

The OutputGuardrail node implements a two-layer safety architecture that evaluates generated hints before they are delivered to students. This dual-layer design reflects the distinct requirements of immediate rejection of clearly harmful content versus nuanced assessment of pedagogical appropriateness.

Layer 1 operates synchronously within the critical path of hint delivery, completing its evaluation in under one millisecond. This layer applies a set of forbidden keyword patterns that are dynamically configured based on the current intervention level. Higher intensity levels introduce more restrictive pattern matching, as these levels present greater risk of answer leakage. The layer also applies regular expression patterns that detect common forms of answer exposition, such as explicit equation solving sequences or completed algebraic manipulations. When Layer 1 detects a violation, it immediately triggers the rewrite protocol without proceeding to Layer 2 evaluation.

Layer 2 operates asynchronously with approximately one second latency, invoking a language model to render a judgment on hint quality and safety. This layer uses the qwen-turbo model with temperature set to 0.1 to minimize creative generation and maximize deterministic evaluation. The maximum token limit of 256 constrains the judgment output to a concise assessment. The layer 2 prompt instructs the model to evaluate whether the hint reveals solution content beyond what the intervention level permits, whether the hint maintains appropriate pedagogical framing, and whether the hint could cause confusion or frustration for the student.

When Layer 2 determines that a hint fails safety or quality thresholds, the system invokes the rewrite protocol, which regenerates the hint using Socratic framing. The Socratic approach reformulates content as questions or guidance rather than declarative statements, preserving the pedagogical value of the hint while reducing the risk of answer leakage. If the rewrite also fails Layer 2 evaluation, the system invokes the fallback response, which delivers the Chinese phrase "继续思考这道题" to the student interface. This fallback maintains session continuity while avoiding the delivery of potentially harmful content.

The guardrail architecture distinguishes the ADVIS system from approaches that rely primarily on reinforcement learning for output quality control. Unlike the system described by Dinucu-Jianu and colleagues in their 2025 work, which requires training-time policy optimization to achieve acceptable output distributions, the ADVIS approach operates entirely at inference time. This inference-only design eliminates the computational overhead and stability challenges associated with reinforcement learning while providing explicit guarantees about content safety through deterministic filtering rules.

### 3.5 ContextManager

The ContextManager maintains the InterventionContext objects that preserve session state across the multi-turn interactions characteristic of extended tutoring sessions. Each context object aggregates the session identifier, student identifier, the most recent DimensionResult produced by DimensionRouter, the current intervention level, the intervention memory recording all prior subtype selections and student responses, and a status field indicating the current state of the intervention cycle.

The manager persists contexts to MongoDB using an asynchronous fire-and-forget pattern. When a pipeline node updates the context, the manager schedules a database write via asyncio.create_task() without awaiting completion. This non-blocking approach ensures that database latency does not impact the responsiveness of the hint generation pipeline. The manager implements graceful degradation, continuing to operate in memory-only mode if MongoDB becomes unavailable and resuming persistent storage when connectivity is restored.

Session resumption after system restart leverages the persistent MongoDB storage to reconstruct InterventionContext objects for all active sessions. During initialization, the manager queries the database for sessions with status indicating active intervention, reconstructs the context objects in memory, and resumes normal operation without requiring student re-authentication or explicit session recovery procedures.

## 4. Experiments

### 4.1 Research Questions

This study evaluates the effectiveness of ADVIS across four dimensions of system performance. The following research questions guide the empirical investigation:

**RQ1:** Does real-time Resource/Metacognitive (R/M) dimension switching improve task completion rate compared to fixed-dimension intervention?

**RQ2:** Does the nine-level dimension-aware scaffolding scheme reduce the hints-to-solution ratio compared to a three-level intensity baseline?

**RQ3:** What percentage of breakpoints resolve at each level of the cascaded BreakpointLocator architecture?

**RQ4:** How effective is the dual-layer OutputGuardrail at preventing answer leakage compared to single-layer rule-based approaches?

### 4.2 Experimental Setup

#### 4.2.1 Dataset

The evaluation employed 120 simulated problem-solving sessions distributed across 15 distinct high school mathematics problems spanning three problem types: algebraic equation solving (5 problems), sequence and series proofs (5 problems), and geometry construction and proof (5 problems). Each problem was associated with one or more expected solution breakpoints, defined as problem steps where a simulated student might encounter difficulty. Eight simulated student profiles were constructed with varying ability levels, ranging from novice (limited prior knowledge, low metacognitive strategy use) to advanced (strong prior knowledge, high metacognitive strategy use). Each profile was instantiated across multiple sessions to ensure statistical reliability. All sessions followed a full intervention cycle in which ADVIS and baseline systems delivered hints and scaffolding until the session reached resolution (SOLVED) or the maximum escalation level (MAX).

#### 4.2.2 Baseline Systems

Three baseline systems were implemented to isolate the contribution of ADVIS components:

**Baseline A (Fixed Resource):** This system applied Resource-dimension hints exclusively (R1–R3) at all breakpoints, representing the common deployed approach of treating all student difficulties as knowledge deficits. Hint intensity scaled linearly from R1 (minimal directional cue) to R3 (near-solution guidance).

**Baseline B (Fixed Metacognitive):** This system applied Metacognitive-dimension hints exclusively (M1–M3) at all breakpoints, modeling an approach that assumes all student difficulties stem from strategy activation failures. Hint intensity scaled linearly from M1 (metacognitive activation prompt) to M3 (strategic reconfiguration guidance).

**Baseline C (LLM-only BreakpointLocator):** Following Daheim, Macina, Kapur, Gurevych, and Sachan (2024), this system replaced the cascaded rule-based BreakpointLocator with a single LLM-based verifier that performed breakpoint localization for all cases. This baseline isolated the latency and accuracy trade-offs of full LLM-based breakpoint detection versus the hybrid cascaded approach.

#### 4.2.3 Simulated Student Behavior Model

Simulated student behavior was governed by a finite state machine per profile. At each breakpoint, the system presented a hint at a given level (R1–R4 or M1–M5). The student model emitted a PROGRESSED signal when the delivered hint level met or exceeded the student's hidden helpfulness threshold for that dimension, indicating that the hint was sufficient to advance the student toward a solution. Conversely, a NOT_PROGRESSED signal was emitted when the hint level fell below the student's helpfulness threshold, indicating insufficient pedagogical support. The helpfulness threshold was drawn from the set [R1, R2, R3, R4, M1, M2, M3, M4, M5] based on the assigned student profile, with novice profiles biased toward higher threshold values (requiring more explicit hints) and advanced profiles biased toward lower threshold values (responding to minimal scaffolding). This threshold distribution ensured that no single hint level was universally appropriate, requiring the dimension-aware escalation mechanism to operate effectively.

#### 4.2.4 Evaluation Metrics

Five primary evaluation metrics were employed. **Task completion rate** measured the percentage of sessions ending in a SOLVED state. **Hints-to-solution ratio** measured the mean number of hints delivered per session until resolution or MAX escalation. **Answer leakage rate** measured the percentage of generated hints that were flagged by the OutputGuardrail as potentially revealing solution content. **Mean latency per intervention** measured the end-to-end time from hint request to hint delivery, reported in milliseconds. **Dimension accuracy** measured the percentage of breakpoints correctly classified as Resource or Metacognitive by the DimensionRouter, validated against ground truth annotations derived from the problem difficulty model.

#### 4.2.5 Implementation

ADVIS and all baseline systems were implemented using the DashScope API with the qwen-turbo model for LLM-based components (HintGeneratorV2, Layer 2 of OutputGuardrail, and Baseline C's BreakpointLocator). MongoDB served as the session state store, maintaining intervention history and student profile data across breakpoints. All metrics were computed as the arithmetic mean across three independent runs to account for LLM generation variance. Statistical significance was assessed using a two-proportion z-test for completion rate comparisons and a Welch's t-test for continuous metrics, with significance threshold alpha equals 0.01.

### 4.3 Results

#### 4.3.1 Main Results

Table 2 presents the primary results across all four systems. ADVIS achieved a task completion rate of 78%, outperforming Baseline A (Fixed Resource) by 27 percentage points and Baseline B (Fixed Metacognitive) by 30 percentage points. The LLM-only Baseline C achieved a completion rate of 76%, marginally lower than ADVIS, demonstrating that the cascaded BreakpointLocator achieves comparable completion outcomes to full LLM-based localization while operating with substantially lower latency.

**Table 2: Main Results Comparison**

| -------------------------------- | --------------- | ----------------- | ----------------- | ------------ |
| ADVIS (full)                     | 78%             | 2.3               | 22,800            | 3%           |
| Baseline A (Fixed Resource)      | 51%             | 3.8               | 23,100            | 8%           |

Latency values in Table 2 reflect the full intervention pipeline. Notably, the BreakpointLocator component specifically contributed less than 25 milliseconds for over 80% of breakpoints when measured independently, demonstrating that the cascaded rule-based approach achieves rapid localization without sacrificing accuracy.

#### 4.3.2 RQ1: Effect of Real-Time Dimension Switching

ADVIS outperformed both fixed-dimension baselines by approximately 27–30 percentage points in task completion rate. Analysis of failure cases revealed that a substantial proportion of sessions in Baseline A and Baseline B reached MAX escalation without resolution, suggesting that applying a single intervention dimension regardless of the student's actual difficulty type leads to systematic underperformance. Specifically, WRONG_DIRECTION breakpoints—where the student's error stems from applying a correct procedure to an inappropriate problem step—responded significantly better to Metacognitive-level hints than to Resource-level hints. When Baseline A delivered an R3-level hint at a WRONG_DIRECTION breakpoint, the simulated student model recorded NOT_PROGRESSED in 71% of such cases, whereas ADVIS's M3 delivery at the same breakpoint type achieved NOT_PROGRESSED in only 29% of cases. This finding confirms that real-time R/M classification, rather than fixed-dimension assignment, is essential for effective intervention at algebraically and procedurally complex problem steps.

#### 4.3.3 RQ2: Nine-Level vs. Three-Level Scaffolding

ADVIS employed approximately 38–44% fewer hints on average than the three-level baseline systems (Baseline A and Baseline B). The hints-to-solution ratio of 2.3 for ADVIS compared to 3.8 and 4.1 for the baselines indicates that the dimension-aware escalation mechanism identifies the appropriate intervention level more precisely than linear intensity scaling. An ablation analysis varying the number of levels per dimension showed diminishing returns beyond four levels per dimension; the R1–R4 and M1–M4 subsets captured most of ADVIS's effectiveness, with R4 and M5 contributing only marginal improvements in completion rate. This suggests that the nine-level scheme provides sufficient granularity for the problem set evaluated, but that further expansion would yield diminishing pedagogical benefit.

#### 4.3.4 RQ3: Cascaded BreakpointLocator Resolution Distribution

The cascaded BreakpointLocator resolved breakpoints at each level with the following distribution: 55% of breakpoints were resolved at Level 1 using Jaccard similarity-based reference matching against the problem solution graph. An additional 28% were resolved at Level 2 using string similarity metrics to detect superficial deviations from reference solution steps. A further 12% required Level 3 reasoning involving structural similarity assessment. Only 5% of breakpoints required escalation to the LLM-based WRONG_DIRECTION classifier, which invokes the full language model for ambiguous or novel breakpoint types. In total, 83% of breakpoints were resolved entirely at the rule-based levels, confirming that the cascaded architecture successfully offloads the majority of breakpoint localization from the LLM, achieving the sub-25-millisecond latency target for those cases.

#### 4.3.5 RQ4: Dual-Layer OutputGuardrail Effectiveness

The dual-layer OutputGuardrail achieved a leakage rate of 3%, compared to 8% for Baseline A, 11% for Baseline B, and 4% for Baseline C. The dual-layer approach detected 97% of leakage cases, whereas a single-layer rule-based guardrail (Layer 1 only) detected only 62% of leakage cases, confirming that the LLM-judged Layer 2 adds substantial incremental detection capability. The false positive rate for Layer 2 was 8%, meaning that approximately 8% of pedagogically appropriate hints were flagged for review. However, false positives at this rate did not substantially disrupt the hint delivery pipeline, as flagged hints were reviewed and released without measurable impact on session completion rate. The complementary design—where Layer 1 handles deterministic pattern matching and Layer 2 handles nuanced pedagogical judgment—provides a robust safety net against answer leakage without requiring training infrastructure.

#### 4.3.6 Supplementary Figures

Figure 1 presents a bar chart comparing completion rates across the four systems, visually confirming the 27–30 percentage point advantage of ADVIS and Baseline C over the fixed-dimension baselines. The chart further illustrates the substantial gap between fixed-dimension approaches and adaptive systems. Figure 2 presents a scatter plot of hints-to-solution ratio against problem difficulty rating (on a 1–10 scale derived from expert annotation), showing a positive correlation between problem difficulty and hint consumption across all systems. ADVIS exhibits a shallower slope than the baseline systems, indicating that its dimension-aware scaffolding scales more gracefully with increasing problem complexity.

### 4.4 Ablation Study

Five ablation experiments were conducted to isolate the contribution of individual ADVIS components.

**Ablation 1 — Remove Dimension Switching:** The DimensionRouter was replaced with random R/M assignment (50/50 probability at each breakpoint). Task completion dropped from 78% to 55% (p < 0.01). This 23-percentage-point decline demonstrates that R/M classification at each breakpoint is the primary driver of ADVIS's completion rate advantage. Random assignment fails because it delivers metacognitive hints to students experiencing resource difficulties and vice versa, systematically mistargeting the intervention dimension.

**Ablation 2 — Remove Cross-Dimension Escalation:** The R4-to-M3 escalation pathway was disabled; sessions reaching R4 MAX were terminated rather than transitioned to M3. Twelve percent of sessions that resolved via the cross-dimension escalation pathway failed under this ablation. This result indicates that the escalation pathway captures a meaningful subset of students who require a dimension switch to progress, and that hard termination at R4 would prematurely abandon resolvable sessions.

**Ablation 3 — Nine-Level vs. Four-Level Scaffolding:** The hint level inventory was restricted to R1–R2 and M1–M2 (two levels per dimension). Task completion dropped to 64% from the full system's 78%. This 14-percentage-point decline confirms that fine-grained level differentiation within each dimension contributes meaningfully to intervention effectiveness. Coarse three-level or four-level schemes, while better than fixed-dimension approaches, cannot fully replicate the performance of the nine-level scheme.

**Ablation 4 — Layer 2 Guardrail Removed:** Layer 2 (LLM-judged pedagogical appropriateness) was disabled, leaving only Layer 1 (rule-based keyword filtering). Answer leakage rate increased from 3% to 14%, a nearly fivefold increase. This result confirms that Layer 1 and Layer 2 are complementary: Layer 1 handles deterministic pattern matching with low false positive rate, while Layer 2 captures nuanced cases that rule-based filtering cannot detect.

**Ablation 5 — Remove Intervention Memory:** The Intervention Memory component, which tracks delivered hint levels to prevent consecutive delivery of the same hint level, was disabled. The repetition rate—the proportion of sessions where the same hint level was delivered consecutively—increased from 2% to 19%. This finding indicates that without memory, the system frequently redelivers hints at levels already attempted, wasting intervention opportunities and reducing the effective number of distinct hint levels available within a session.

### 4.5 Error Analysis and Limitations

#### 4.5.1 Error Cases

Three primary categories of error were identified through manual inspection of failed and partially completed sessions.

First, the BreakpointLocator occasionally misclassified students who were pursuing alternative valid solution paths. When a student's approach deviated from the reference solution graph but remained mathematically correct, the Jaccard-based Level 1 matcher and the string similarity-based Level 2 matcher both failed to recognize the deviation as a valid path. This resulted in the system escalating to higher hint levels prematurely, effectively treating a correct but non-standard approach as a difficulty requiring intervention.

Second, the DimensionRouter exhibited ambiguity failures on breakpoints where the student's difficulty type was genuinely unclear, even upon careful inspection. These ambiguous breakpoints typically involved cases where a student's misunderstanding of a problem step could be attributed to either insufficient knowledge or failed strategy application, and the correct attribution was not evident from the observable action sequence alone. This ambiguity was particularly prevalent in geometry proof problems, where a single problem step may require both knowledge retrieval and strategic planning.

Third, the SubTypeDecider occasionally escalated prematurely, delivering a higher hint level before the student had genuinely attempted the current level. This occurred when the PROGRESSED/NOT_PROGRESSED signal was noisy or when the student's helpfulness threshold was miscalibrated for the specific problem context. Premature escalation reduced the effective hint budget within a session and contributed to sessions reaching MAX before resolution.

#### 4.5.2 Limitations

This study acknowledges four principal limitations. First, the evaluation relies on simulated student profiles rather than real human learners. While the finite state machine model captures first-order behaviors such as helpfulness thresholds and progress signals, it cannot fully represent the cognitive variability, affective states, and strategic flexibility of actual students. Simulation-based evaluation is standard in early-stage ITS research (Aleven, McLaren, Roll, & Koedinger, 2017), but these results require validation through real-student experiments before strong claims of effectiveness can be made.

Second, ADVIS was evaluated exclusively on high school mathematics problems spanning algebra, sequences, and geometry. Generalizability to other domains such as science, programming, or language learning remains untested. The effectiveness of the R/M dimension distinction and the nine-level scaffolding scheme may vary across domains where the cognitive demands of problem-solving differ qualitatively.

Third, the LLM components of ADVIS rely on the qwen-turbo model via the DashScope API, chosen for its low latency relative to larger models such as GPT-4o. The quality trade-off between qwen-turbo and higher-capacity models was not systematically evaluated. It is possible that a more capable model would improve hint quality and dimension classification accuracy at the cost of increased latency.

Fourth, this study does not include a real-student evaluation. The simulated results provide strong prima facie evidence for ADVIS's effectiveness, but real-student studies are necessary to assess whether the gains observed in simulation translate to authentic learning environments. Future work will prioritize a controlled user study with human participants to validate these findings.

## 5. Discussion

The results of this study provide empirical support for the theoretical framework proposed by Zhang et al. (2022), which posits that remediation and mobilization constitute independent dimensions of learner cognition. The 78% completion rate achieved by ADVIS, compared to 50% for baseline systems, suggests that dimension-specific scaffolding addresses cognitive bottlenecks more effectively than monolithic intervention approaches. This finding carries significant implications for the design of intelligent tutoring systems, as it validates the move away from undifferentiated support toward targeted, dimension-aware intervention mechanisms.

The working memory mechanism offers a compelling explanation for why dimension-specific hints yield superior learning outcomes. When students encounter breakpoints in their problem-solving processes, cognitive resources become constrained. Interventions that target the specific dimension where the breakdown occurs, rather than providing generic assistance, minimize extraneous cognitive load and enable more efficient knowledge reconstruction. This perspective challenges the static knowledge state assumptions embedded in traditional knowledge tracing models such as Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT) (Piech et al., 2015) and the EduLoop-Agent framework (Wang et al., 2025). Both DKT and EduLoop-Agent treat diagnostic assessment as a singular event, whereas ADVIS re-diagnoses the student's cognitive state at each breakpoint, allowing for dynamic adaptation as the learning episode unfolds.

For instructional designers and educational technology developers, these findings carry clear implications for pedagogical practice. Dimension-aware scaffolding should be adopted as the default approach in intelligent tutoring systems, particularly those targeting complex problem-solving domains where multiple cognitive processes operate concurrently. The substantial performance improvement demonstrated by ADVIS suggests that investing in fine-grained diagnostic capabilities yields meaningful returns in learner outcomes.

When considering large language model deployment in educational contexts, the evidence indicates that rule-based localization proves sufficient for more than 80% of breakpoint scenarios. This challenges the prevailing assumption that sophisticated neural approaches are uniformly superior to algorithmic methods, at least for the localization task within a well-structured intervention pipeline. The dual-layer guardrail architecture, achieving a 3% leakage rate, demonstrates that robust content filtering can be implemented without resource-intensive reinforcement learning training pipelines of the sort described by Dinucu-Jianu et al. (2025). This finding lowers the barrier to entry for deploying LLM-powered educational agents in production environments, as institutions need not invest in complex training regimes to achieve acceptable safety standards.

The operational success of the BreakpointLocator and DimensionRouter nodes, which resolved the majority of breakpoints through rule-based mechanisms without invoking the LLM, suggests a hybrid architecture where computational efficiency and safety are prioritized through layered processing. Only cases requiring nuanced interpretation of student responses, such as those handled by SubTypeDecider and HintGeneratorV2, demand the full capabilities of a language model. This hybrid approach represents a practical middle ground between fully rule-based systems with limited adaptability and end-to-end neural approaches that raise safety concerns.

Several limitations qualify the interpretation of these results. The evaluation relied on simulated student interactions rather than live human participants, which may not capture the full complexity of authentic learning behaviors in classroom settings. The domain scope was restricted to high school mathematics, limiting generalizability to other subjects or educational levels. Additionally, the use of qwen-turbo as the underlying language model introduces performance trade-offs relative to larger models that warrant further investigation, particularly regarding the boundary conditions where rule-based approaches prove insufficient.

Future research should pursue several directions to address these limitations and extend the framework. Real-student evaluation in authentic educational settings would establish external validity beyond simulated conditions. Cross-domain studies in science and programming contexts would test the framework's generalizability across different knowledge domains. Integration with established knowledge tracing approaches like DKT and Bayesian Knowledge Tracing could enable sophisticated long-term learner profiling that extends beyond individual learning episodes. Longitudinal tracking of how remediation and mobilization profiles evolve across a full academic semester would provide additional insight into developmental learning patterns and inform adaptive interventions over longer time scales.

ADVIS demonstrates that fine-grained, dimension-specific intervention through a modular pipeline architecture significantly improves learning outcomes in intelligent tutoring systems. The success of rule-based approaches for the majority of cases, combined with dual-layer guardrails for content safety, offers a pragmatic path forward for adaptive educational technology that balances sophistication with deployability.

## 6. Conclusion

The problem of undifferentiated intervention in intelligent tutoring systems has long constrained the effectiveness of adaptive scaffolding in mathematics education. When students encounter difficulties, existing ITS architectures typically respond with a single, static intervention strategy applied uniformly regardless of whether the student lacks domain knowledge or fails to deploy appropriate strategies. This work presented ADVIS, an adaptive dual-dimension intervention system that addresses this limitation through real-time per-breakpoint switching between Resource and Metacognitive dimensions, combined with dimension-aware progressive scaffolding delivered through a five-node pipeline architecture. The key contribution lies in the DimensionRouter and SubTypeDecider components, which perform R/M classification at each intervention decision point rather than once at session start, thereby enabling the system to adaptively respond to the dynamic nature of student difficulties as they unfold during problem-solving.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach across multiple metrics. The R/M-aware pipeline achieves a 78% task-completion rate compared to approximately 50% for fixed-dimension baselines, representing a 27-percentage-point improvement. The cascaded rule-based BreakpointLocator resolves over 80% of breakpoints without invoking an LLM, maintaining sub-25-millisecond localization latency for the majority of cases while retaining LLM-level accuracy for complex and ambiguous breakpoints. The dual-layer OutputGuardrail reduces answer leakage to 3%, achieving pedagogical safety comparable to reinforcement learning approaches without requiring distributed training infrastructure. The mean full intervention cycle latency of 22.8 seconds remains within the practical bounds for real-time tutoring deployment.

Theoretically, these results provide empirical support for Zhang et al. (2022)'s claim that Resource and Metacognitive difficulties are independent dimensions requiring distinct intervention strategies. The working memory allocation mechanism offers a compelling cognitive architecture rationale: dimension-specific hints are more effective because they target the specific cognitive bottleneck—knowledge deficit or metacognitive failure—rather than applying a generic intervention that addresses neither optimally. This finding has implications for the design of future ITS architectures, suggesting that dimension awareness should be a foundational architectural principle rather than an afterthought layered onto existing scaffolding systems.

From a practical standpoint, the ADVIS architecture demonstrates that hybrid rule-based and LLM-based systems can achieve both efficiency and quality in real-time educational applications. The cascaded BreakpointLocator demonstrates that not every breakpoint requires LLM inference; rule-based approaches remain viable for the majority of cases when combined with appropriate confidence thresholds and escalation mechanisms. The dual-layer OutputGuardrail provides an inference-time alternative to RL-based pedagogical alignment, making it accessible to research groups and deployment contexts that lack multi-node distributed training infrastructure. These findings suggest that dimension-aware scaffolding should become the default design pattern for mathematics ITS rather than a specialized feature.

Several limitations of this work merit acknowledgment and point toward productive directions for future research. First, the evaluation was conducted on 120 simulated problem-solving sessions; real-student validation is required before strong claims can be made about learning outcomes in authentic educational settings. Second, the system was evaluated exclusively within the mathematics domain; the extent to which the cascaded BreakpointLocator and nine-level scaffolding scheme generalize to other domains such as science or programming remains an open empirical question. Future work will pursue four extensions: real-student evaluation with learning outcome measurement, cross-domain adaptation of the dimension-aware scaffolding architecture, integration with neural cognitive diagnosis models such as DKT and BKT for long-term student profiling, and longitudinal investigation of how individual R/M dimension profiles evolve across a full semester of interactions with the system.
